## 3.1 What Is DeepVerify Pro? (Product Overview)

DeepVerify Pro is an enterprise-grade real-time deepfake detection and content authenticity platform that combines live audio and video synthetic media detection with cryptographic verification. It addresses the core vulnerability exposed by the Arup incident – the complete absence of any mechanism to verify whether call participants are genuinely human and whether communications originate from trusted sources. By merging the real-time detection capability of DeepVerify with the cryptographic signing infrastructure, the combined platform protects organisations against both live deepfake calls and tampered pre-recorded communications simultaneously.
DeepVerify Pro is designed for large enterprises especially companies like Arup, financial institutions, law firms, and government agencies where video and voice calls are regularly used to authorise high-value transactions or sensitive decisions. The primary users are employees in finance, legal, and executive roles – precisely the individuals targeted in the Arup fraud. Unlike existing tools such as Pindrop and Reality Defender which focus exclusively on audio analysis or require post-call review, DeepVerify Pro provides live multi-modal detection combined with proactive content signing, creating a two-layer defence that neither solution could achieve alone.

## 3.2 Why Combine Ideas 1 and 5?

DeepVerify alone had one limitation – it could only detect deepfakes during a live call. If an attacker sent a pre-recorded deepfake video message or a synthetic audio clip rather than conducting a live call, DeepVerify would have no opportunity to analyse it in real time.
ProvenanceShield alone had a different limitation – it could only verify communications that originated from within the organisation's own signed systems. External callers impersonating internal staff, as in the Arup case, would carry no valid internal signature, making the tool ineffective against the most common cyberattacks.
Together they eliminate both weaknesses. DeepVerify Pro detects synthetic content in real time during live calls, while ProvenanceShield's cryptographic signing layer flags any communication – live or pre-recorded, internal or external – that lacks a verified provenance signature. An attacker would need to simultaneously defeat both a live neural network detection engine and a cryptographic signing infrastructure – a significantly higher barrier than either system presents alone.

## 3.3 Key Features

Feature 1 – Real-Time Audio Deepfake Detection (ACM 1.2, 1.3)
DeepVerify Pro analyses every caller's voice continuously throughout a live call, extracting mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCCs) – a digital fingerprint of the unique tonal qualities of a human voice – every 25 milliseconds. These features are compared against patterns known to originate from AI voice synthesis tools including ElevenLabs, Resemble AI, and Tacotron using a light convolutional neural network (LCNN). The system displays a live colour-coded confidence indicator visible to the user throughout the call – green meaning the voice is verified genuine, amber meaning uncertain, and red meaning the voice is likely synthetic. In the Arup case, the cloned executives’ voice would have triggered a red alert within seconds of the call beginning, before any financial instructions were communicated.
Feature 2 – Live Video Face Authenticity Verification (ACM 1.3, 1.6)
Simultaneously with audio analysis, DeepVerify Pro analyses the video stream of each participant by tracking 68 facial landmark points – key positions on the face including the eyes, nose, jawline, and mouth corners. Real human faces exhibit natural micro-movements, irregular blinking patterns, and subtle skin imperfections. AI-generated faces consistently produce detectable artifacts including unnatural smoothness, lighting inconsistencies, and irregular blinking frequency. A convolutional neural network trained on thousands of genuine and synthetic face samples scores each video frame in real time. In the Arup case, the AI-generated CFO and fabricated colleagues on the video call would have failed this check immediately, as synthetic faces cannot replicate the full complexity of natural human facial behaviour.
Feature 3 – Cryptographic Content Provenance Signing (ACM 2.5, 3.7)
Building on ProvenanceShield's C2PA-based infrastructure, DeepVerify Pro embeds an invisible cryptographic signature into all video and audio communications produced within the organisation at their point of origin. Any communication received – whether a live call, a pre-recorded video message, or an audio clip – is automatically checked for a valid provenance signature. Communications lacking a valid signature are immediately flagged as potentially synthetic or tampered, regardless of whether they come from internal or external sources. This feature addresses the limitation of the original ProvenanceShield concept by extending provenance verification beyond internal communications to flag the absence of any trusted signature on incoming content. In the Arup case, the deepfake call would have carried no valid provenance signature, triggering an automatic alert before any financial discussion occurred.
Feature 4 – Out-of-Band Financial Authorisation Trigger (ACM 1.2, 2.5)
When DeepVerify Pro detects financial language during a call – such as requests for wire transfers, account numbers, or payment approvals above a defined threshold – it automatically sends a separate verification request to the requester's registered device through an independent channel completely outside the call environment. No financial transaction can be processed until this out-of-band confirmation is completed on the requester's separate registered device. This feature ensures that even in the unlikely event that both the audio and video detection engines fail to flag a sophisticated deepfake, no financial authorisation can be completed based on a single call alone. In the Arup case, this single feature would have broken the fraud chain at the very first of the 15 fraudulent transactions.
Feature 5 – Audit Trail and Incident Reporting (ACM 3.1, 3.7)
Every call and communication processed through DeepVerify Pro generates a timestamped, tamper-proof log of all detection events, risk scores, provenance checks, and flagged moments. This audit trail serves three purposes: it enables internal security teams to investigate incidents immediately after they occur, it provides law enforcement with forensic evidence of when and how synthetic content was used, and it gives insurers the documentation required to process fraud claims. The anonymised detection data is also used to continuously improve the platform's neural network models as new deepfake techniques emerge, ensuring the system evolves alongside the threat. In the Arup case, a complete audit trail would have significantly accelerated the Hong Kong police investigation and provided clear forensic evidence for the subsequent legal proceedings.

## 3.4 How It Works Technically

DeepVerify Pro operates through three parallel pipelines running simultaneously during every processed communication.
The audio pipeline captures the caller's voice and extracts MFCC features every 25 milliseconds, feeding them into a light convolutional neural network (LCNN) trained on both human speech and AI-generated voice samples. The network outputs a continuous probability score indicating whether the voice is synthetic, which updates the live confidence indicator in real time throughout the call.
The video pipeline analyses each incoming video frame using a separate convolutional neural network trained to detect facial inconsistencies typical of deepfake generation – unnatural skin texture, irregular blinking, lighting mismatches, and micro-expression irregularities. The model tracks 68 facial landmarks per frame and flags deviations from natural human facial behaviour patterns that are invisible to the human eye but consistently detectable by trained neural networks.
The provenance pipeline operates at the communication infrastructure level, embedding C2PA-compliant cryptographic signatures into all outgoing communications at their point of origin and automatically verifying incoming communications for valid signatures before they reach the recipient. This pipeline runs independently of the audio and video detection engines, providing a separate layer of protection that does not depend on the quality or sophistication of the synthetic content being detected.
All three pipelines run as a lightweight background plugin integrated via the Zoom and Microsoft Teams developer APIs. The entire analysis process occurs on the organisation's own servers – no audio, video, or communication data is transmitted to external third-party servers – protecting organisational privacy and complying with GDPR, the Australian Privacy Act, and ACM principle 1.6.
Users see only a simple colour-coded indicator throughout the call requiring no technical knowledge to interpret, ensuring the tool is accessible to all employees regardless of technical background.

## 3.5 Product Impact

Immediate benefits by stakeholders group:
The direct benefits of DeepVerify Pro are not uniform across all stakeholders – they address the specific harms identified in the ERF analysis for each group differently.
For finance employees – the group bearing the greatest personal burden in the Arup incident – DeepVerify Pro removes the impossible burden of detecting sophisticated deepfakes through human judgment alone. The real-time colour-coded indicator and the financial authorisation trigger mean that no single employee can ever again be placed in the position of sole decision-maker for a multi-million dollar transfer authorised through a video call. The psychological harm of being deceived while acting in good faith, and the subsequent professional consequences, are precisely the harms this system is designed to prevent at the point before they occur.
For impersonated executives, DeepVerify Pro's cryptographic content provenance signing directly addresses the privacy violation identified under ACM 1.6. Because every legitimate internal communication is signed at its point of origin, any attacker attempting to weaponise an executive's face or voice against their own organisation will produce content that carries no valid signature. The executive's identity is no longer something that can be harvested and redeployed without leaving a technically detectable trace – the cryptographic layer restores a degree of control over one's own professional identity that deepfake technology had effectively eliminated.
For Arup as an institution (and organisations like it), the audit trail and incident reporting feature directly addresses the reputational and legal exposure that follows a fraud event. A timestamped, tamper-proof record of detection events significantly reduces the time and cost of post-incident investigation and provides insurers and law enforcement with usable evidence – a gap that was painfully evident when Hong Kong police investigated the Arup case with no digital forensic trail from the call itself.
For Arup's clients and business partners, the deployment of DeepVerify Pro functions as a credible signal that the organisation has addressed its communication security vulnerabilities. This matters because, as identified in the stakeholder analysis, clients suffered an indirect harm – diminished confidence in whether communications from Arup could be trusted – that no financial recovery alone could repair.
Risk analysis of DeepVerify Pro:
It would be dishonest to present DeepVerify Pro as a risk-free intervention, albeit it still runs on the power of AI, and there are three considerations to be made when implementing DeepVerify Pro.
The automation complacency risk.
As the system becomes embedded in daily workflows, there is a genuine risk that employees begin to treat a green indicator as an absolute guarantee of authenticity rather than a probabilistic assessment. If the neural network's confidence score reads green, employees may stop applying independent judgment entirely – effectively transferring human duties to an automated system that, however accurate, operates within a specific set of training assumptions. DeepVerify Pro does mitigate this by design – the financial authorisation trigger activates regardless of the detection score, ensuring that no automated green-light can single-handedly authorise a high-value transaction. However, we acknowledge that this mitigation is partial: for non-financial deception scenarios outside the trigger threshold, the overreliance risk remains real and would need to be addressed through complementary employee training that the system alone cannot replace.
The adversarial escalation effect.
Deploying a detection tool at scale creates a commercially visible target for adversarial improvement. Criminal organisations that develop deepfake tooling will, over time, specifically test their outputs against the detection signatures that systems like DeepVerify Pro rely on. This is not a hypothetical situation – similar cyberattacks like spam filters and spam generators over the preceding two decades represent a vivid example of how fast adversarial technology can be developed. The audit trail's anonymised aggregation of detection data is designed to accelerate the defensive side of this cycle, but the arms race dynamic cannot be eliminated. It can only be managed through continuous model retraining and active threat intelligence sharing – neither of which is guaranteed once the platform is deployed in organisational environments that may deprioritise update cycles.
The false positive cost.
 A system that flags genuine calls as potentially synthetic will, at some rate, generate false positives. For time-sensitive business contexts – a CFO confirming a legitimate acquisition from an airport lounge on a poor connection, for example – a red alert triggering an out-of-band authorisation requirement may introduce friction that affects real business operations. If false positive rates are not communicated transparently, organisations may disable detection features to remove friction, eliminating the protection entirely. This is a deployment and UX design challenge as much as a technical one, and it requires honest calibration of sensitivity thresholds across different organisational contexts.
Scalability: Realistic Conditions and Barriers:
The existing Product Solution section correctly identifies that DeepVerify Pro's underlying technology is adaptable beyond large enterprise use. However, scaling this technology involves barriers that should not be understated.
Technical barrier:
The on-premise processing architecture – designed to avoid transmitting audio and video to external servers – requires organisations to maintain sufficient local compute infrastructure to run three parallel neural network pipelines in real time. For large enterprises like Arup, this is feasible. For small and medium businesses, or healthcare providers operating on smaller infrastructure, the hardware requirement represents a realistic adoption barrier. A cloud-processing alternative with appropriate data handling agreements would be required, but introduces the privacy trade-offs the on-premise design was built to avoid. This is a complicated tension which does not have a clean solution.
Regulatory barrier:
Extending DeepVerify Pro into government identity verification or legal proceedings – two of the highest-value use cases – requires formal regulatory recognition that cryptographic signatures are admissible evidence of communication authenticity. No such standard currently exists in most jurisdictions, especially ones that are based on AI. Achieving it would require many discussion steps with legal bodies that could potentially be processed throughout years, not months. It is not that it is impossible – it is just that DeepVerify’s detection architecture has to be top-notch that needs to be accurate up to whichever sigma level the court wants.
Adoption barrier:
The tool's effectiveness scales directly with the proportion of an organisation's communications that pass through its detection infrastructure. If only the finance department deploys it, attackers will simply shift their targeting to communications routed outside that department. Meaningful protection requires organisation-wide deployment, which in turn requires executive sponsorship, employee training, and sustained IT change management – precisely the organisational conditions that the Arup case demonstrated were absent when they were needed most.
We acknowledge that these projections involve uncertainty. The rate at which deepfake detection models can keep pace with generation improvements, the regulatory timeline for cybersecurity standards and the organisational willingness to allocate budget for mass deployment are all variables that cannot be predicted with confidence. What can be stated with confidence is that the specific harms identified in the ERF analysis – an employee deceived through no fault of their own, an executive's identity weaponised without consent, and an organisation exposed to a potential catastrophic financial and privacy loss through a single unverified communication – are directly addressed by this product solution in ways that none of the existing tools reviewed were designed to address simultaneously.
